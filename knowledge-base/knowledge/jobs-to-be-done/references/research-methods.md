# Research Methods for JTBD

JTBD analysis is only as good as the research behind it. This reference covers three primary interview methods and practical guidance for conducting JTBD research.

---

## When to Use Each Method

| Method                    | Best For                                                                  | Effort Level                              |
| ------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| Switch Interviews         | Understanding why people change solutions; demand generation              | Low (30–45 min each, 8–12 interviews)     |
| Outcome-Driven Interviews | Building a comprehensive need set for a known job; roadmap prioritization | Medium (60–90 min each, 12–20 interviews) |
| Contextual Inquiry        | Discovering jobs you didn't know existed; understanding workarounds       | High (2–4 hours each, 6–10 sessions)      |

Start with switch interviews if you're exploring. Move to outcome-driven interviews when you've committed to a job. Use contextual inquiry when the job is complex and hard to articulate.

---

## Method 1: Switch Interviews (Moesta)

Switch interviews reverse-engineer the motivation behind a recent decision to change solutions. They reveal the forces that drive adoption and the jobs that customers are hiring a product to do.

### Who to Interview (Switch)

People who recently:

- Started using your product (what were they "firing"?)
- Stopped using your product (what did they "hire" instead?)
- Evaluated your product but chose something else
- Made a significant change in how they get the job done

### The Four Forces Framework

Every switch decision involves four forces. Probe all four:

1. **Push of the current situation** — Frustration with the existing solution
   - "What was going wrong with what you were using before?"
   - "When did you first realize it wasn't working?"
   - "What was the specific moment that made you think 'I need to find something else'?"

2. **Pull of the new solution** — Attraction to the alternative
   - "What about the new solution caught your attention?"
   - "What did you imagine it would be like to use it?"
   - "What specific outcome were you hoping for?"

3. **Anxiety of the new solution** — Fear about switching
   - "What concerns did you have about making the change?"
   - "What could have gone wrong?"
   - "What almost stopped you from switching?"

4. **Habit of the current situation** — Inertia and comfort
   - "What was good about what you had before?"
   - "What made you hesitate to change?"
   - "What would you miss about the old way?"

### Switch Interview Flow

1. **Set the scene** (5 min): "I'd like to understand how you came to use [product/approach]. Can you take me back to when you first started thinking about making a change?"
2. **Timeline** (15 min): Walk backward from the moment of purchase/adoption. When did they first think about it? What triggered the search? What did they consider? What did they try?
3. **Forces** (15 min): Probe each of the four forces. Get specific stories, not abstract opinions.
4. **Outcome** (5 min): "Now that you've been using it, has it delivered on what you expected? What's different from what you imagined?"

### What to Listen For

- The "trigger event" — the specific moment that started the search
- The "hiring criteria" — what mattered most in the decision
- Workarounds and compensating behaviors — things they do that the product doesn't solve
- Unspoken emotional and social dimensions — how they felt, who noticed

## Method 2: Outcome-Driven Interviews (Ulwick)

Outcome-driven interviews systematically uncover the desired outcomes at each stage of the job process. They produce the inputs needed for quantitative prioritization via the Opportunity Algorithm.

### Who to Interview (Outcome-Driven)

People who regularly perform the core functional job — the practitioners, not their managers or the purchasers. Aim for 12–20 interviews across a representative mix of heavy users, light users, and people who struggle with the job.

### Outcome-Driven Interview Flow

1. **Define the job** (10 min): Confirm the core functional job. "I understand you're responsible for [job]. Can you describe what that involves?" Validate the job statement.

2. **Walk the job map** (40–60 min): For each stage of the job (Define → Locate → Prepare → Execute → Monitor → Modify → Conclude → Share), ask:
   - "What are you trying to accomplish at this stage?"
   - "How do you know when this stage is going well?"
   - "What could go wrong here?"
   - "What would 'perfect' look like?"
   - "How do you measure success at this point?"

3. **Capture outcomes** (ongoing): As the interviewee describes what they're trying to achieve, translate into desired outcome format:
   - Direction + measure + object + clarifier
   - Example: "I want to make sure nothing falls through the cracks" → "Minimize the likelihood that a task is overlooked during handoff between team members"

4. **Explore emotional and social jobs** (10 min):
   - "How do you feel when this job is going well? When it's going badly?"
   - "Who notices the quality of your work on this job?"
   - "How do you want to be perceived in relation to this work?"

5. **Capture circumstances** (5 min):
   - "Under what conditions does this job become significantly harder?"
   - "When does it matter the most to get this right?"

### Tips for Outcome Capture

- Aim for 50–150 outcomes across all job stages for a thorough analysis
- Even a lightweight pass should yield 15–30
- Each outcome should be atomic (one concept only)
- Avoid solutions in outcome statements — if someone says "I need a notification," translate to "Minimize the time it takes to learn that a dependent task has been completed"
- Capture the outcome in the moment, then refine the wording later

## Method 3: Contextual Inquiry

Contextual inquiry combines observation and interview in the participant's real work environment. It reveals jobs, workarounds, and friction that people can't articulate in a conference room.

### Who to Observe

People performing the job in their natural setting. Visit their office, their home, their workspace. See their actual tools, their actual process, their actual interruptions.

### What to Watch For

- **Workarounds**: Sticky notes, side spreadsheets, manual calculations, duplicate entry — these signal unmet needs
- **Tool switching**: How many different apps, windows, or tools do they touch to complete the job? Each switch is potential friction.
- **Interruptions and recovery**: How do they handle being interrupted mid-job? How long does it take to get back on track?
- **Emotional signals**: Sighing, re-doing work, expressed frustration, visible pride — note these with timestamps
- **Things they don't do**: Steps they skip, shortcuts they take, quality checks they bypass because they're "not worth it"

### Interview During Observation

While watching, ask:

- "What are you doing right now? Why?"
- "Is this how you usually do it, or is today different?"
- "What would happen if you skipped this step?"
- "Show me what happens when [a common complication] occurs"

### Capturing Insights

Take photos (with permission) of their workspace, screens, and artifacts. These are powerful in stakeholder presentations and help the team build empathy with the job performer.

## Practical Guidance

### Sample Size

| Research Goal                           | Minimum Interviews | Ideal   |
| --------------------------------------- | ------------------ | ------- |
| Exploratory (what jobs exist?)          | 6–8                | 12–15   |
| Validating a specific job               | 8–12               | 15–20   |
| Building a full outcome set             | 12–15              | 20–30   |
| Quantitative survey (after qualitative) | 100+ respondents   | 200–600 |

Diminishing returns on qualitative research typically kick in around 12 interviews — you start hearing the same things.

### Recruiting Participants

- Recruit actual job performers, not proxies (not managers describing what their team does)
- Include a range: heavy users, light users, people who struggle, people who excel
- If studying switching behavior, recruit people who switched within the last 90 days (memory fades fast)
- Avoid recruiting only enthusiasts or power users — they're not representative

### Common Research Mistakes

- **Asking about features instead of jobs**: "What features would you want?" will give you a feature wishlist. "Walk me through your process when you need to [job]" will give you jobs and needs.
- **Asking leading questions**: "Don't you think it would be better if...?" biases responses. Ask open-ended questions and follow the participant's lead.
- **Interviewing in groups**: Individual interviews reveal honest experiences. Groups create social dynamics where people agree with the loudest voice.
- **Not following up on emotional signals**: When someone says "that's really frustrating," don't move on. Ask "tell me more about that — what specifically is frustrating? When does it feel the worst?"

### When You Can't Do Research

Sometimes budget, time, or access constraints make primary research impossible. In those cases:

1. **Mine support tickets and NPS comments** for job-related language
2. **Interview customer-facing teams** (sales, support, customer success) about what they hear repeatedly
3. **Analyze competitor reviews** on G2, Capterra, or app stores — negative reviews reveal unmet needs
4. **Run a lightweight survey** with open-ended questions: "Describe the last time you tried to [job]. What was the hardest part?"

Label the output clearly as "hypothesized" and plan to validate with direct research when possible.
